# Disruption Index Framework

## Overview
The **Disruption Index** is a set of composite indexes designed to measure the frequency, intensity, and impact of disruptions across various settings and domains of life. The framework is modular, allowing for both broad and granular analysis.

---

## **1. Core Domains**

### **A. Environmental Settings**
| Category          | Subcategory          | Description                                                                 | Example Metrics                                                                 |
|-------------------|----------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

|                   | Digital              | Disruptions via digital means (e.g., hacking, doxxing, online harassment)  | Cyberattacks, data breaches, online stalking incidents                         |
| **Outdoors**      | Public Spaces        | Disruptions in public or shared outdoor areas                               | Vandalism, protests, natural disasters                                         |
|                   | Travel/Commuting     | Disruptions during travel or commuting                                      | Traffic delays, transport strikes, accidents                                   |

## **2. Disruption Types**

### **A. Intentional Disruptions**
- **Malicious Actions**: Deliberate attempts to disrupt (e.g., sabotage, harassment, cyberattacks).
- **Obstruction**: Active blocking of progress or access (e.g., denial of resources, legal threats).

### **B. Unintentional Disruptions**
- **Environmental**: Natural or infrastructural (e.g., weather, power outages).
- **Technological**: Failures or limitations of tools/systems (e.g., software bugs, hardware failures).

### **C. Systemic Disruptions**
- **Policy/Regulatory**: Changes in laws or policies causing instability (e.g., Brexit, new compliance rules).
- **Organizational**: Internal changes in organizations (e.g., layoffs, restructuring).

---

## **3. Measurement Axes**

| Axis               | Description                                                                 | Example Metrics                                                                 |
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Frequency**      | How often disruptions occur                                                 | Daily, weekly, monthly counts                                                   |
| **Intensity**      | Severity or impact of each disruption                                       | Low (minor inconvenience) to High (life-altering)                                |
| **Duration**       | How long disruptions last                                                   | Minutes, hours, days                                                            |
| **Scope**          | Breadth of impact (individual vs. group vs. systemic)                      | Number of people affected, geographic spread                                   |
| **Recovery Time**  | Time required to return to baseline after disruption                         | Hours/days to recover productivity, well-being, or stability                     |

---

## **4. Composite Indexes**

### **A. Domain-Specific Indexes**
- **Home Disruption Index (HDI)**: Aggregates disruptions in the home environment.
- **Outdoor Disruption Index (ODI)**: Aggregates disruptions in outdoor/public settings.
- **Personal Well-being Disruption Index (PWDI)**: Focuses on sleep, rest, and mental health.
- **Professional Disruption Index (PDI)**: Measures disruptions in work and networking.

### **B. Cross-Domain Indexes**
- **Overall Disruption Index (ODI)**: Combines all domain-specific indexes for a holistic view.
- **Intentional Disruption Index (IDI)**: Tracks only malicious or obstructive disruptions.
- **Systemic Disruption Index (SDI)**: Focuses on policy, organizational, or large-scale disruptions.

---

## **5. Scoring Methodology**

### **A. Weighting**
- Assign weights to each subcategory based on importance (e.g., sleep quality may weigh more than leisure interruptions).
- Example: Sleep (30%), Work Focus (25%), Home Safety (20%), Networking (15%), Outdoor (10%).

### **B. Normalization**
- Scale all metrics to a common range (e.g., 0–100) for comparability.

### **C. Aggregation**
- Use weighted averages or geometric means to combine subcategory scores into domain indexes.
- Example: **PDI = (0.4 × Focus Disruptions) + (0.3 × Tool Disruptions) + (0.3 × Networking Disruptions)**

---

## **6. Visualization & Reporting**

### **A. Dashboards**
- Real-time tracking of disruption scores across domains.
- Heatmaps to identify high-disruption periods or areas.

### **B. Alerts**
- Threshold-based alerts for severe or escalating disruptions.

### **C. Trends**
- Time-series analysis to identify patterns (e.g., seasonal, weekly, or daily trends).

---

## **7. Use Cases**

### **A. Personal**
- Track individual disruption levels to identify stressors and improve well-being.
- Example: "My PWDI spiked in Q2 due to sleep disruptions from noise pollution."

### **B. Organizational**
- Monitor employee productivity and well-being.
- Example: "Team PDI increased after the new policy rollout; investigate root causes."

### **C. Societal**
- Assess community or regional stability.
- Example: "City ODI rose 20% after the infrastructure failure; prioritize repairs."
